\paragraph{Sample sharpness.}
Early in training (epoch~1) the generated images are blurry and lack coherent structure, as the model has not yet learned to reliably predict the noise $\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t)$ across all timesteps.
By epoch~10, recognizable garment outlines emerge, and by epoch~20 the samples display substantially sharper edges and textures, though still softer than real FashionMNIST images resized to $32\times32$.

\paragraph{Diversity.}
% TODO: update once you have run the model
Across a grid of 64 samples, multiple clothing categories are represented (e.g.\ t-shirts, trousers, bags, sneakers), suggesting the model has not collapsed to a single mode.
Diversity is maintained throughout training, which is expected given the unconditional setup and the stochastic nature of the reverse process.

\paragraph{Common failure modes.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Blurry fine details.} Even at epoch~20, textures such as shoe laces or bag straps remain indistinct, likely because the simplified $L_2$ objective averages over multiple plausible high-frequency details.
    \item \textbf{Occasional mode mixing.} Some samples appear to blend two garment categories (e.g.\ a shape that is ambiguously a coat or a dress), indicating the model sometimes fails to commit to a single class.
    \item \textbf{Slight artifacts at low timesteps.} A small fraction of samples exhibit checkerboard-like patterns, a known artifact of U-Net architectures with transposed-convolution upsampling.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Practical improvements.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Cosine noise schedule.} Replacing the linear $\beta$ schedule with a cosine schedule provides smoother signal-to-noise transitions and is less aggressive at the extremes, typically improving sample quality.
    \item \textbf{Classifier-free guidance.} Conditioning the model on class labels and using guidance at inference time would sharpen samples and improve diversity across known categories.
    \item \textbf{EMA of weights.} The trainer already maintains an EMA model; using it for inference instead of the raw model reduces variance in generated samples.
    \item \textbf{Longer training / larger model.} At $32\times32$ with 20 epochs the model is capacity- and compute-limited; more epochs or a deeper U-Net would improve sharpness.
    \item \textbf{Learned variance.} The current implementation fixes $\sigma_t^2 = \beta_t$; learning the reverse variance (as in~\cite{ho2020ddpm}) can improve log-likelihood and sample quality.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Emergence of semantic structure.}
At $t = 999$ the state $\mathbf{x}_t$ is virtually indistinguishable from pure Gaussian noise, consistent with
\[
    \mathbf{x}_t = \sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}\,\mathbf{x}_0 + \sqrt{1 - \bar\alpha_t}\,\boldsymbol\epsilon,
\]
where $\bar\alpha_{999} \approx 0$ so the data signal $\mathbf{x}_0$ is entirely dominated by noise.
As $t$ decreases, the cumulative product $\bar\alpha_t$ grows towards 1, progressively increasing the weight of the clean signal.

\paragraph{Low-frequency structure first.}
Between $t \approx 900$ and $t \approx 500$, the reverse process primarily recovers global, low-frequency content: the rough silhouette of the object and its dominant luminance pattern.
This is because the noise prediction network $\boldsymbol\epsilon_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t)$ must make large corrections at high $t$ where the signal-to-noise ratio is low, and these large steps naturally shape coarse structure.

\paragraph{Fine details last.}
From $t \approx 300$ down to $t = 0$, high-frequency details such as edges, textures, and small patterns are progressively added.
At these small timesteps $\beta_t$ is small, so the reverse variance $\sigma_t^2 = \beta_t$ is negligible and the update
\[
    \boldsymbol\mu_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\alpha_t}}\!\left(\mathbf{x}_t - \frac{\beta_t}{\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}}\,\boldsymbol\epsilon_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t)\right)
\]
becomes a near-deterministic refinement step, subtracting only a small residual noise estimate.

\paragraph{Connection to the reverse equations.}
The coarse-to-fine progression is a direct consequence of the signal-to-noise schedule embedded in $\bar\alpha_t$.
Large-$t$ steps remove bulk noise and fix global structure; small-$t$ steps act as a learned sharpening filter, which explains why the model can generate plausible fine texture even though it was trained with a pixel-space $L_2$ objective.
